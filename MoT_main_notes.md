# Conducting Exploratoring Testing
[How to conduct a session](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Wu4xTnoreLY)

![Risks and Questions 5 step Plan](/Images/exploratory_step_plan.png)

The goal is to find issues that may not be covered by pre-existing test cases, so you need to come up with different ways to test the feature.
As software features are implemented, and user stories become ready for exploratory testing, it’s only logical to base exploratory
testing sessions on new stories or changed features. Good exploratory testing deals with unexpected risks, and for this
we need to look beyond the current piece of work. On the other hand, we can’t cast the net too widely, because testing would
lack focus. A good perspective for investigations that balances wider scope with focus is around user capabilities. Features provide
capabilities to users to do something useful, or take away user capabilities to do something dangerous or damaging. A good way
to look for unexpected risks is not to explore features, but related capabilities instead.

# How to make it work
To get to good capabilities for exploring, brainstorm what a feature
allows users to do, or what it prevents them from doing. When exploring user stories, try to focus on the user value part (‘In order
to…’) rather than the feature description (‘I want …’). If you use impact maps for planning work, the third level of the map
(actor impacts) are a good starting point for discussing capabilities. Impacts are typically changes to capabilities. If you use user story
maps, the top-level item in the user story map spine related to the current user story is a nice starting point for discussion.

Good exploratory testing requires good note-taking skills. It looks good and I found this bug might be enough. 
But what are you missing out on by not taking notes to support your summary? 
- What information have you left undiscovered? 
- What did you observe that you didn’t document? 
- What haven't you shared?

Regardless of your note-taking tool these four simple words will keep your notes organised:

- Problems
- Questions
- Ideas
- Praise

![PQIP](/Images/PQIP.png)

Grab a physical notepad and pen or open up an application that allows you to type notes, such as Google Docs, Word or Evernote.

Pick something to test. Maybe the latest deploy includes a story that needs your attention. 

Set a timer for 30 minutes.

Start exploring! 

Document in real-time what you’re thinking and observing.

Try to categorise each note as either a problem, question, idea or praise. Don't worry if they don’t fall into one of those categories, 
just leave the categorisation blank.

Stop exploring at 30 minutes.

### Exploratory testing-related artefacts
Systematic exploratory testing involves noting down various artefacts such as:

* Charters: A brief document that defines the objectives and scope of an exploratory testing session. 
These help testers know more about the areas to focus on without any constraints.

* Goals: A high-level definition of what a tester aims to achieve during exploratory testing. 
These might include identifying specific bugs, validating assumptions, or testing high-risk areas of the application.

* Notes: During exploratory testing, testers take notes on their findings, observations, or new ideas. 
These notes can later be refined and transferred to official artefacts for further analysis.

# A Holistic Look At Usability Testing 

It can be thought of as being comprised of five quality components:

* **Learnability**: The design should be intuitive. How easy is it for users to accomplish
basic tasks the first time they use the product? Do they make any errors? Do they get frustrated with the application?

* **Efficiency**: Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they perform tasks? If they struggle with what should be a straightforward task, why is this? Can the number of clicks a user has to make to complete a task be reduced?

* **Memorability**: When users return to the design after a period of not using it, how easily can they reestablish proficiency?

* **Errors**: How many errors do users make, how severe are these errors, and how easily can they recover from the errors?

* **Satisfaction**: How pleasant is it to use the design? Does the user like using the application?

# Six thinking hats
- White hat - what facts do we know and what information do we need? 
- Yellow hat - what are the positives, values and benefits? What occurs when we look at the situation with optimism? 
- Black hat - what are the problems and risks? Potentially the most powerful hat but you need to be careful not to over-focus on this one. 
- Red hat - what do you feel about the situation? Do you have any hunches, likes, dislikes, loves or hates? 
- Green hat - what are the possibilities? Do we have any new ideas, concepts, perceptions or alternatives? 
- Blue hat - what do we think about this? It is also where we control the techniques process and ensure the guidelines are followed.

### How to use testing personas in practice
Testing personas can add focus to exploratory testing sessions. By using personas, testers can step into the mindsets of specific user types, helping them think beyond typical scenarios and find issues real users might face. This approach lets testers adapt their testing style based on each user’s needs, leading to more practical, user-centred results.

* `Set the stage for each test`: Before you start, assign a persona to the session. This way, you’re testing as if you were that user. For instance, if you’re testing for the casual user, check if error messages are clear and if too many options might be confusing.

* `Test realistic workflows`: Focus on workflows that match how each persona would actually use the product. Ask yourself:
   -  What does this persona want to do with this feature?
   -  Is anything in the design confusing or slowing them down?

* `Learn and improve`: After testing with each persona, review what you found. Did the casual user get stuck? Did the advanced user find the feature too slow? Use these findings to make simple, focused improvements.

An example: Testing personas for Facebook’s remember me feature
In lean testing, you focus on the value to the customer, and personas help you pinpoint that value. Let’s look at how personas could be applied to a common feature like “Remember Me” in a login system.

* `Persona 1`: Busy professional
Goal: Save time by not having to log in repeatedly.
Challenges: They have little patience for errors.
Test Focus: Ensure “Remember Me” keeps them logged in seamlessly, without frequent re-authentication.

* `Persona 2`: Privacy-conscious user
Goal: Wants security without hassle.
Challenges: Worried about personal data being saved.
Test Focus: Ensure that “Remember Me” doesn’t bypass security requirements when switching devices or browsers.

In regulated environments, it's crucial to use clear, precise, and scenario-specific language in test reports and documentation to avoid ambiguity. This involves avoiding generalized statements and instead specifying the conditions and scenarios under which tests were conducted. 

**Examples of unsafe language:**
- “It works!”
- “All good”
- “No bugs in the product”
- “Passes as Expected”

**Examples of safe language:**
- “It appears to work under the following conditions …”
- “When tested on the following kinds of scenarios …”
- “Using the following versions of tools, scenarios, etc.”

**Tests for special conditions include:**
- Handling data correctly when users do not accept cookies.
- Implementing disaster recovery in both production and development systems.
- Validating accessibility aspects like sufficient contrast, alt-text for images, screen reader support, and navigation aids.
- Testing for backup, rollback, access control, network security, and processes.
- Ensuring role-based access controls, such as preventing the same person from issuing and authorizing a payment, and tying business and regulatory rules to the software's access control mechanisms.

# Ask useful questions:
Lena suggests using questions like “What would happen if…?” to explore potential risks and reveal assumptions and gaps in understanding. Questioning assumptions and challenging vague areas in the artefacts can lead to discovering hidden risks that might otherwise go unnoticed.

# Spot gaps and assumptions:
Gaps in requirements or unclear assumptions are often a source of risk. Look for missing information or implicit expectations in the artefacts. Lena uses the iceberg analogy to explain this: the explicit requirements are above the waterline, but many hidden assumptions lie beneath. Recognising and addressing these assumptions is crucial for thorough risk analysis.

# Take relevant notes:
Note-taking is one of the most valuable tools for testers. Write down potential risks, assumptions, and follow-up questions as you analyse artefacts. These notes help you stay organised and ensure you don’t overlook important points. You can use them later to guide discussions with stakeholders, developers, or other team members.

# Focus on priorities:
Not all risks are equal. Prioritise areas with the highest potential impact or greatest uncertainty for the context of your project. For example, legal compliance (such as GDPR), user experience, or scalability issues might demand more attention. Identifying these critical areas helps you allocate your efforts effectively.

# Use simple models:
Creating diagrams or sketches can help you visualise how the system works and identify potential risks and failure points. Lena demonstrates how to map out processes like order flows, from the user placing an order to generating a work order or invoice. Simple models make it easier to understand complex systems and highlight areas that need closer examination.

### Systems thinking Approach

In its most simplistic description, it is an approach for looking at the system as a whole rather than just the individual components. 
How do each feature or function interact, connect and contribute to the overall workings of the system? 
By viewing the whole system we can theorise that a change in one part may have unintended consequences. 
 

#### A helpful analogy as a final thought

If a software system were an orchestra, then each musician would be a component. Each component would have different instruments. 
The music would be the system in action and the music would work best when all the musicians are playing in the same tune in sync. 

The tester listens to the system (systems thinking) to ensure they are in harmony and sounding good.
They might listen to individual instruments to make sure everything is ok, but it’s the rich sound of the full orchestra that tells us
everything is as it should be.

### Why models matter
What is a model? Simply put, it’s a simpler version of something complicated. 
Think of a map, it doesn’t show every tree, hill or crack in the road, but it gives you enough information to get where you’re going.

As a tester, having a model helps to:

* Understand boundaries:

Knowing what’s included and what’s not helps you focus on the right things. 
For example, if you’re testing a banking app, are you looking at the entire app or just one feature? 
If you’re clear on the boundaries, you won’t be distracted by areas that aren’t the immediate focus. 

* Find risks:

 Knowing where problems might occur through understanding behaviour helps you test smarter. 
 For example, a feature that a lot of people use, or one that was just changed is more likely to have issues.
 When you find risky areas, you can focus your testing there and avoid last minute surprises.

* Generate test ideas: 

Thinking about both positive and negative scenarios helps you plan your tests. 
For example, if you’re testing file uploads, you might ask:

	Am I informed of progress and completion? 
	Is it easy to understand how to submit a file? 
	What happens if a file is too big?
	How does it handle unsupported file types?
	What happens if the internet is slow?
	These questions come from understanding how the system works and where it might fail.


### Breaking down your system
 
*_Step 1_* : List the parts
To build a model, you start by identifying the key pieces of the system. 
For a social media platform, these might include:

- User’s: People who interact with the platform by creating, sharing, or reacting to content.
- Content: Anything users create or share, what they react to, adding videos or stories.
- Algorithms: Decide what content to show users based on preferences, activity, or trends.
- Notifications: Alerts that bring users back to the platform, like when someone reacts to their content or follows them.
- Community: Groups or spaces where users with similar interests connect and share.
- Moderation: Systems or processes to manage inappropriate or harmful content and ensure platform rules are followed.
- Engagement: How users interact with the platform, such as likes, comments, shares, or reactions.
- Search: Helps users find content, profiles, or topics of interest.
- Security: Making sure the user's personal details are not taken without consent. 

*_Step 2_* : Map the relationships
Once you know the parts, think about how they interact. Ask yourself:

What triggers each part?
What parts or actions depend on which others?
Where could something go wrong?
Here’s how it might look for a social media platform:

A user creates a post.
The algorithm decides if the post appears in their followers’ feeds.
Notifications alert other users when someone likes or comments.
At this point, you can use something simple like a mind map from lesson 3 to start organising your ideas. 
Draw out the parts and how they connect. It doesn’t have to be perfect, just enough to see the relationships.

*_Step 3_* : Build the model

What is behavioral modeling?
Behavioral modeling is a model type that focuses on identifying and defining the dynamic behavioral aspects of software components. The goal is to represent how software functions, features, and system elements behave when in operation.

Behavioral models generally take one of three basic forms:

State machine representations – These models depict software behavior in terms of defined states that the software or components can be in, along with events that can trigger transitions between those states. State machine modeling is valuable for elucidating complex workflows, protocols, lifecycles, and user interaction flows.
Control flow models – These behavioral models illustrate software behavior through sequences of events, triggers, and messages that cause software processes to be dynamically activated or deactivated over time. Control flows are useful for expressing orchestration and coordination logic.
Data flow models – This form represents software behavior in terms of how data moves through various processes, transformations, and mappings in route to its ultimate destination in data stores or data sinks. Data flow modeling helps clarify throughput and signal processing behavior.
Simulation tools provided in many software modeling environments give the software engineer a way to dynamically step through and interactively test behavioral models and prototypes. Being able to visualize and validate the modeled behavior in action helps verify that the software’s different constituent parts work together per the intended interaction design.

### Module 8
Lesson 1: What are requirements, and why we should care?
 
**User story**:
A clear and concise description of the product that focuses on user value.
Commonly followed structure syntax: 
As a [role], I want [feature] so that [benefit].

Example user story: As a user, I want to be able to apply filters based on my criteria
so that the items can be listed accordingly.

**Acceptance criteria**:
Define what "done" or “completed” looks like for a requirement, user story or task.
Serve as a checklist of must-haves for completion. Aligns developers, testers, and other stakeholders
on shared expectations. Ensure that the requirement is verifiable and testable. They also act as a mini-requirement
that supports and clarifies the overall goals. Commonly followed structure syntax: 

Given [something / status / setup data], When [action / event], Then [defined outcome]. 
Example acceptance criteria for user story: 
Given the user applies a filter, the results update dynamically.
When no results match the filter, a “No results found” message is displayed.
Then, the filter reset option appears.

**Features**
Features are logical sets of business values that include multiple user stories.
It’s like a collection of related user stories under a common theme.
Example feature: The "Search" functionality may contain filters, recommendations, and previous searches made.
Example acceptance criteria for search feature:

- The search bar is accessible on all pages.
- Filters, recommendations, and previous searches work seamlessly together.
- The system can handle at least 100 concurrent searches without performance degradation.

In the above example, filters, recommendations, and previous searches are all independent stories
that make up the search feature. While features are often defined first in Agile projects and then 
broken down into user stories, we have begun with user stories and acceptance criteria to illustrate how requirements
are structured before scaling up to a feature.

**Epic**
It is a large requirement that is broken down into multiple stories or features sharing a common relationship.
Epic helps in breaking down a big requirement into small actionable chunks. Example: An "Account Management" 
epic could potentially include stories like resetting passwords and updating profile information.


## Based on focus areas
* Business requirements
Requirements aligned with high-level organisational goals and needs.
These requirements are about value to the business.
Example: “Increase user retention by 15% over the next quarter.”

* User requirements
Requirements centred on user needs, expectations, desires, and convenience.
These requirements are about value to the user.
Example: “Free users can view their last three orders.”

* Technical requirements
Requirements that focus on technical implementation details.
These requirements talk about changes to the code or software.
Example: "Develop a predictive text search using ElasticSearch technology."

### The Golden Rules of Requirements
In requirement wisdom, this means that requirements;

Are not complete
Are usually a representation of the writer’s interpretation of the actual requirement, rather than the actual truth
Are not equal to or only the requirement documents 
Are never captured explicitly in requirement artifacts 
May be mentioned in passing, discussed during meetings, or just assumed. Don’t ignore them when they appear.

Remember from lesson one we can ask “... and how will we test that?”. As a tester, it’s important to look 
and ask for testability whenever we are dealing with any new requirements. If a requirement isn’t testable, 
ask for clarification or measurable details.

### Clarifying requirements 
**1. Spot vague phrases:** Ambiguous wording in requirements are very common, as highlighted by Maddy, Emily, and Philippa. For example, phrases like “reasonable time frame” appear frequently in performance requirements. But what’s reasonable depends entirely on context. For an archiving system, waiting minutes might be acceptable, but for a streaming platform, even a few seconds could be too long. Without clear definitions, teams risk costly rework and a poor user experience. Look out for words like reasonable, fast, or efficient that lack concrete benchmarks.

**2. Use techniques to break down meaning:** Emily shared the “Mary had a little lamb” technique, where each word in a requirement is analysed to uncover possible variations and hidden assumptions. Applying this approach helps testers challenge vague language and refine requirements. Similarly, Scott used a technique of asking different people on the team how they interpret a requirement, revealing their biases and assumptions. This helped highlight gaps in understanding and ensured requirements were reviewed from multiple perspectives.

**3. Ask targeted questions to remove ambiguity:** Scott encountered the requirement “payments cannot be over ten thousand” and had to clarify whether this applied to pounds or euros, per account or per person. He asked multiple people—business analysts, product owners, project managers, system architects—before finally getting a clear answer from legal and compliance. Escalating questions to the right people is crucial for resolving ambiguity.
Find gaps using opposites and assumptions: Philippa explained that even a simple user story like “As a user, I want to verify my account” might be missing details. She demonstrated how flipping the statement to its opposite (e.g., what happens if the user cannot verify their account?) and questioning assumptions (e.g., is the user new, existing, or deleted?) helps uncover missing information. Applying this method helps teams uncover potential issues early.

**4. Don’t assume—clarify!** – All four testers stress the importance of questioning ambiguous words and assumptions. Talking to product owners, business analysts, or even the legal teams can ensure requirements are precise and testable, reducing the risk of confusion later in the project. There is a powerful connection between the ability to ask questions and the ability to talk about risk that wil positively influence your approach to testing. So not only are you clarifying requirements, you’re also developing your question asking skills. It’s a win-win!

## Step 1: Start with what the requirement says
Testers have to know what the requirement is stating or implying about the system's behavior, functionality, or constraints. So let's look more closely at the requirements.

Next, ask yourself: “Why is this requirement important?”

Is it solving a user problem?
Is it mitigating a risk?

Ask questions

Does the system handle unexpected inputs?

* What is a valid email?

### Step 4: Apply clarifying understanding 
Let’s try to clarify our understanding with a seemingly simple requirement.

Requirement: “The system must evaluate passwords for strength.”

#### Steps:
What does “strength” mean?
This is currently quite vague and meaningless. Let’s clarify the requirement.
Rewritten requirement:

- Upon submission, the frontend validates that the password meets complexity rules (minimum 8 characters, has at least one uppercase and lowercase character and a special character !@£$%&*^+). On completion of frontend validation being successful the password will be passed to the server for final validation. Either failure results in the system displaying the error message, “Your password does not meet our complexity rules of xxx”. (Note, even better would be to have the complexity rules showing before you start to create a password). Even a simple requirement can involve multiple steps and systems working together:

* The client-side validation ensures users get immediate feedback. Client-side refers to items displayed and things that take place on the end user device.
* The server-side validation double-checks for security. Server-side refers to everything that happens on the server side, such as interacting with databases.
* The error message needs to clearly guide the user on what to fix. By rewriting the requirement like this, you can identify all the moving parts that need testing—from client-side validation to server response and error messaging.

	* Data-driven testing: Validate a wide range of inputs and outputs using scripts.
	* Continuous integration testing: Automate the software delivery pipeline to verify builds when new code is added.

### Lesson 1 - Module 10

#### The impact of preparation in testing

Poor preparation in testing can lead to:

* Missing bugs due to testing the wrong version
* Wasted time due to incorrect environment setup
* Incomplete testing due to lack of proper access or tools
* Inefficient use of testing time due to unclear objectives
* Missed requirements due to a lack of understanding of the context

Before you begin testing, you need to ensure you're testing the right thing in the right place. This means verifying:

### Software version
Software versions often have a unique identifier or build number and can also have names. A common version naming conversion is Major, Minor and Patch for significant, standard and bug fixes. 

- Confirm the exact version number or build to test
- Verify deployment status and location
- Check if any hotfixes or patches need to be applied
- Understand which features are included in the version

### Environment setup
How an environment is setup depends a lot on the system but generally includes things like configuring hardware, ensuring tools, languages, databases etc. are appropriate to needs. These are most often set up for teams but sometimes testers can be involved. 

- Verify the test environment matches the required specifications
- Confirm the environment is available and ready for testing
- Check all necessary services and dependencies are running
- Ensure the environment is isolated from other testing activities

### Tools and access
To test something we need to be able to access it. For some systems this can be very simple and in others, more likely in regulated and controlled industries, it can be very complicated involving service desk requests and multiple teams. 

- Required testing tools are installed and configured
- Test management system access is set up
- Bug tracking system access is verified
- Monitoring tools are available if needed
- Access to logs, databases and debugging tools is confirmed

### Platform considerations
What we are testing and why can give direction to what platforms we will need to test on. These can be areas like web browsers, operating systems, mobile devices or desktop based. 

- Required devices are available and prepared
- Browser versions are installed and updated or access to virtual machines is available
- Screen sizes and resolutions are available for testing
- Mobile devices are charged and have necessary apps installed

### Test objectives
Test objectives or goals are the specific measurable outcomes for a test session. They can be as varied as the software that can be created and will rely on the context of testing. 

- Understand what needs to be tested
- Understand the test coverage requirements
- Identify any areas out of scope
- Have clear goals for the testing session

### Time, people and resource planning
Too often how long we get to test, the people assigned to a project and what other resources are available to us are out of our hands. This does not mean we don’t need to consider these factors and raise any issues or risks associated with them. 

- Estimate the time required for different testing activities
- Plan breaks to maintain focus and effectiveness
- Account for setup and documentation time
- Allow a buffer for unexpected issues and interruptions
- Understand the team and the different ways they can help or support testing

### Understanding the context testing is taking place in
Testing doesn't happen in a vacuum. Understanding the broader context is crucial for successful testing. That context can be anything from business goals, time pressures or particular user journeys or perspectives.  

### Project background
Having knowledge of the reasons for the project, its outcomes and goals can help us understand the context of testing requests. 

- Company goals and objectives
- Product vision and strategy
- Success criteria and metrics
- Historical context and previous issues or problem areas

### User analysis
Understanding our user base, how they interact or use software or considering these questions can help us both empathise with users and test with them in mind. 

- Target user personas and characteristics
- Common user scenarios and journeys
- Accessibility requirements and considerations
- Performance expectations

### Stakeholder awareness
Internal and external stakeholders may have more or less influence on a project so it is important that we learn what they need and provide information and insights in the way they prefer. 

- Key stakeholders and their roles
- Communication channels and preferences
- Reporting requirements and formats
- Escalation paths for critical issues

### Risks and priorities
Understanding the risks, both to the business and products or services, gives us valuable information to inform our testing approaches. Through this understanding we can ensure we have our priorities aligned with the project's needs. 

- Business-critical functionalities
- High-risk areas identified from previous releases
- Security-sensitive features
- Performance bottlenecks
- Data integrity concerns
- Time-sensitive features
- Resource constraints and limitations
